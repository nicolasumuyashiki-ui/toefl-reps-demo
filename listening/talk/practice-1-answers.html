🎓 Academic Talk — Animal Migration (Biology)

話者：女性教授 ｜ 約70秒 ｜ 180語Speaker: female professor | ~70 sec | 180 words

📜 スクリプトを見る（シグナルワードをハイライト付き）View script (signal words highlighted)
[NARRATOR] Listen to part of a lecture in a biology class.

[PROFESSOR] So today, let's talk about how some animals find their way during migration. I mean, birds can travel thousands of kilometers, right? And they do it without a map or GPS. So how do they manage?

Well, scientists have found that many birds actually use the Earth's magnetic field as a kind of internal compass. There are tiny particles of a mineral called magnetite in their brains, and these particles react to the magnetic field. So essentially, the bird has a built-in navigation system.

But here's the thing — that's not the only tool they use. Some species also rely on the position of the sun during the day, and stars at night. And younger birds often learn specific routes by following experienced adults on their first journey.

Now, what's really fascinating is that recent studies have shown that light pollution from cities can actually interfere with these natural navigation systems. Birds that fly through heavily lit areas sometimes become disoriented and lose their way.

So next time, we'll look at what conservation efforts are being made to address this problem.

The whole talk covers magnetic fields, sun, stars, and light pollution as navigation methods. (D) "track" refers to scientists tracking — different from the birds' own navigation.

🎓 Academic Talk 2 — Tidal Locking (Astronomy)

話者：男性教授 ｜ 約2分30秒 ｜ 約340語Speaker: male professor | ~2:30 | ~340 words

📜 スクリプトを見る（シグナルワードをハイライト付き）View script (signal words highlighted)
[NARRATOR] Listen to part of a lecture in an astronomy class.

[PROFESSOR] Okay, today let's talk about something I think is one of the most elegant gravitational phenomena in our solar system — tidal locking. Now, if you've ever wondered why the Moon always shows us the same face — why we see the same craters, the same plains, night after night — you're touching on tidal locking.

So what's actually happening? When two objects orbit each other, gravity doesn't just pull on them uniformly. It pulls a little harder on the near side of each object than the far side. That difference in pull is called a tidal force. Over millions of years, this slight imbalance acts like a brake — it slows the rotation of the smaller body until its spin matches the time it takes to orbit the larger one. When that happens, the same hemisphere of the smaller body always faces the larger one. That's tidal locking.

The Moon is the classic example. It rotates exactly once for every orbit around Earth — about every 27 days. So from our viewpoint, the same craters appear in the same spots, forever. The far side of the Moon — sometimes called the dark side, though it's not actually dark — was never seen by human eyes until the Soviet Luna 3 mission photographed it in 1959.

Now, tidal locking isn't unique to the Moon. The dwarf planet Pluto and its moon Charon are mutually locked — they each show the same face to the other. And outside our solar system, astronomers think many exoplanets orbiting close to red dwarf stars are tidally locked, which has huge implications for whether those planets could support life. Imagine a world where one side is in permanent daylight and the other in permanent night — the climate would be radically different from anything on Earth.

We'll come back to that last point next time, when we talk about habitability around red dwarfs.

What is the talk mainly about?
Main Topic
(A) The history of lunar exploration.
(B) A phenomenon that synchronizes the rotation and orbit of celestial bodies.
(C) Future missions to study exoplanets.
(D) The differences between moons and planets.
解説：Explanation: 冒頭の "today let's talk about ... tidal locking" がそのまま主題。後の段落で「同じ面を向ける」現象として synchronize の定義が続く。(A) 月の探査は Luna 3 への一言だけで主題ではない。(C) 太陽系外惑星も主題ではなく一例。The opening "today let's talk about ... tidal locking" sets the topic. The next paragraph defines it as the synchronization of rotation and orbit. (A) Lunar exploration is just a Luna 3 footnote, not the topic. (C) Exoplanets appear only as one example.
🔑 Main Topic問題：冒頭 "let's talk about X" の X がそのまま答え。X を抽象化 (synchronize) しているのが正解。Main Topic rule: the X in "let's talk about X" is the answer. The choice that abstracts X (synchronize) wins.
