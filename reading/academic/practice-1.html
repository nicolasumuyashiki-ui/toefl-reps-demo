The Bystander Effect

In 1964, a woman named Kitty Genovese was attacked outside her apartment building in New York City. According to early reports, dozens of neighbors witnessed the event but none of them called the police. This case shocked the nation and prompted two social psychologists, John Darley and Bibb Latané, to investigate why people sometimes fail to help others in emergency situations.

Through a series of experiments, Darley and Latané discovered what they called the bystander effect. They found that individuals are less likely to offer assistance when other people are present. In one well-known study, participants heard someone apparently having a seizure over an intercom. When participants believed they were the only listener, 85 percent sought help. However, when they thought others were also listening, only 31 percent responded.

The researchers proposed that two psychological mechanisms explain this behavior. The first is diffusion of responsibility, where each person assumes someone else will take action. The second is pluralistic ignorance, in which people look at the reactions of others to decide whether the situation is truly an emergency. If no one else seems alarmed, individuals tend to conclude that help is not needed.

These findings have had a lasting influence on the field of social psychology and have been applied in public safety training programs around the world.
