## SSML script (with break tags per docs/audio-pause-spec.md)

```
Okay, today let's talk about something I think is one of the most elegant gravitational phenomena in our solar system <break time="500ms" /> tidal locking.

Now, if you've ever wondered why the Moon always shows us the same face <break time="400ms" /> why we see the same craters, the same plains, night after night <break time="400ms" /> you're touching on tidal locking.

<break time="600ms" />

So what's actually happening? When two objects orbit each other, gravity doesn't just pull on them uniformly. It pulls a little harder on the near side of each object than the far side. <break time="500ms" /> That difference in pull is called a tidal force.

Over millions of years, this slight imbalance acts like a brake <break time="400ms" /> it slows the rotation of the smaller body until its spin matches the time it takes to orbit the larger one. When that happens, the same hemisphere of the smaller body always faces the larger one. <break time="400ms" /> That's tidal locking.

<break time="600ms" />

The Moon is the classic example. It rotates exactly once for every orbit around Earth <break time="400ms" /> about every 27 days. So from our viewpoint, the same craters appear in the same spots, forever.

The far side of the Moon <break time="400ms" /> sometimes called the dark side, though it's not actually dark <break time="400ms" /> was never seen by human eyes until the Soviet Luna 3 mission photographed it in 1959.

<break time="600ms" />

Now, tidal locking isn't unique to the Moon. The dwarf planet Pluto and its moon Charon are mutually locked <break time="400ms" /> they each show the same face to the other. <break time="500ms" /> And outside our solar system, astronomers think many exoplanets orbiting close to red dwarf stars are tidally locked, which has huge implications for whether those planets could support life. Imagine a world where one side is in permanent daylight and the other in permanent night <break time="400ms" /> the climate would be radically different from anything on Earth.

<break time="600ms" />

We'll come back to that last point next time, when we talk about habitability around red dwarfs.
```
